Ensemble Artistic
Leadership Workshop

Co-designing a shared process for artistic decision-making

~70 minutes  ·  5 phases

Welcome everyone. This workshop is not about deciding everything permanently — it is about agreeing on HOW we want to make artistic decisions together. Keep the energy low-pressure from the start.
Opening

Before we begin

Opening script

"Today is not about deciding everything permanently. It is about agreeing on how we want to make artistic decisions together, so that leadership is shared in a way that supports both trust and artistic quality."

Deliver this slowly. Don't rush past it. If someone tries to solve an artistic question here, name it: "That's important — today we're designing how we'd answer that question, not answering it yet."

What we'll leave with

01
A shared definition of what democratized artistic leadership means in this ensemble
02
A working distinction between collective, consultative, delegated, and director-led decisions
03
A draft working process for making and reviewing artistic decisions
Keep this brief — don't read all three aloud in full. Say "three concrete things" and gesture to them. Move on within 60 seconds.

Phase 1  ·  10 min

Introduction

Leading questions
  • What do we want this ensemble to feel like when leadership is working well?
  • What outcome should we leave with today?
Watch for
  • Whether participants understand the difference between a process discussion and an artistic decision
  • Readiness to engage and shared sense of purpose
The key distinction to establish here: process discussion ≠ artistic decision. If someone jumps to repertoire or interpretation choices, gently redirect: "We'll get to that — this is about how we'd make that call together." Don't let this phase run long. 10 minutes hard stop.
Activity 1  ·  15 min

Values mapping

Step 1  (3 min)

Each person writes 3 values they want the ensemble's artistic leadership to reflect.

Step 2  (5 min)

Share in pairs. Listen for overlap and tension.

Step 3  (7 min)

Plenary share-out. Record recurring themes on whiteboard or shared document.

Ask
  • Which values must be protected in how we make artistic decisions?
  • Which values feel non-negotiable?
Common values that come up: trust, artistic excellence, fairness, flexibility, accountability, courage. WATCH FOR tensions — e.g. speed vs. fairness, artistic vision vs. collective voice. Name these rather than smoothing them over. They are the most useful material in the room. Don't force consensus at this stage. Record divergence, don't resolve it.
Activity 2  ·  15 min

Define decision types

Collective

Whole ensemble decides. Full input and consensus required.

Consultative

Input gathered broadly. One person or small group decides.

Delegated

Authority given to a section lead, role, or individual.

Director-led

Director or appointed lead decides. Speed or expertise required.

← Most shared ownership Most directed →

Together: place common ensemble decisions into these four categories.

Goal isn't to assign every decision permanently — it's to practise the distinction. Good productive tension: where does "interpretation" sit? Probably director-led in a single rehearsal but consultative across a project arc. WATCH FOR: whether the group can separate process from content, and whether they can accept that not every decision needs full consensus.

Examples to sort

Repertoire selection What pieces to perform, and why
Programming order Sequence of works in a concert or set
Interpretation Tempo, phrasing, dynamics, diction, style
Ensemble roles Who leads sections or cues entries
Rehearsal priorities What to focus on and what can wait
Scheduling Rehearsal times, call times, deadlines
Performance format Formation, staging, movement, use of space
Communication norms How feedback is given and disagreement handled
Membership Who joins, covers absences, substitutes
Documentation Scores, notes, shared rehearsal records
Don't rush to correct placements. Disagreement about where something sits is productive — it surfaces assumptions about who holds authority. Print this as a reference card for participants if helpful.
Activity 3  ·  20 min

Scenario testing

Choose 2–3 realistic scenarios

Repertoire choice  ·  Programme order  ·  Stage layout  ·  Contested artistic interpretation

Work through each one:

Who decides?  ·  How is feedback gathered?  ·  How is disagreement handled?

Ask
  • If this happened tomorrow — who speaks, who decides, how is dissent handled?
  • What would make the process feel fair?
This is the richest phase. Let it run over if needed — shorten the consensus check instead, not this. WATCH FOR: confusion about who holds the final call, and whether power imbalances surface. Use past situations if helpful, but don't name individuals — name the situation type. If the group gets stuck, push concretely: "This rehearsal. Tomorrow. Who picks up the baton?"
Activity 4  ·  15 min

Consensus check

✊ Fist Cannot support this
☝️ 1–2 Major concerns
🤟 3 Can live with it
🖖 4 Cautious support
🖐️ 5 Full agreement
Ask
  • Can you live with this process for a trial period?
  • What would you need to feel safe using it?
Capture
  • Full agreements
  • Cautious support with conditions
  • Unresolved concerns
A fist is not a failure — it's information. Surface it: "Can you say more about what's behind the fist?" Don't push for 5s. A room of 3s that feels heard is healthier than a room of 5s that felt pressured. Record all three categories: agreements, cautious support, and unresolved concerns. The last one is what goes into the draft working agreement as "still to resolve."
Closing  ·  10 min

Working agreement + next steps

Before people leave

Summarize agreed principles  ·  Name open questions  ·  Assign someone to draft the working agreement  ·  Set a specific review date

Ask
  • What have we agreed today?
  • What still needs refinement after we try this in practice?
Assign the working agreement drafter BEFORE people leave — vague shared responsibility means no document. The review date must be specific: a rehearsal date, not "in a few weeks." If energy is low, still do this. The closing is where ownership gets assigned, not just felt.
Closing script
Say this to close

"From this session, we now have a draft way of working: what we decide together, what we delegate, and how we handle disagreement. We will test it, revise it, and return to it with evidence from practice."

Deliver slowly. Don't rush to pack up. Let the room settle on what was built today. After you say this, stay quiet for five seconds. Let it land.
