Opening

Today is not about deciding everything permanently. It is about agreeing on how we want to make artistic decisions together, so that leadership is shared in a way that supports both trust and artistic quality.

Deliver slowly. Don't rush past it. If someone tries to solve an artistic question here, name it: "That's important — today we're designing how we'd answer that question, not answering it yet."

Phase 1  ·  10 min

Introduction

Leading questions
  • What do we want this ensemble to feel like when leadership is working well?
  • What outcome should we leave with today?
Watch for
  • Whether participants understand the difference between a process discussion and an artistic decision
  • Readiness to engage and a shared sense of purpose
KEY DISTINCTION to establish early: process discussion ≠ artistic decision. If someone jumps to repertoire or interpretation choices, gently redirect: "We'll get to that — this is about how we'd make that call together." 10 minutes hard stop.
Activity 1  ·  15 min

Values mapping

Step 1  (3 min)

Each person writes 3 values they want the ensemble's artistic leadership to reflect.

Step 2  (5 min)

Share in pairs. Listen for overlap and tension.

Step 3  (7 min)

Plenary share-out. Record recurring themes on whiteboard or shared document.

Ask
  • Which values must be protected in how we make artistic decisions?
  • Which values feel non-negotiable?
Common values that come up: trust, artistic excellence, fairness, flexibility, accountability, courage. WATCH FOR tensions — e.g. speed vs. fairness, artistic vision vs. collective voice. Name these rather than smoothing them over. Don't force consensus at this stage.
Activity 2  ·  15 min

Define decision types

Collective

Whole ensemble decides. Full input and consensus required.

Consultative

Input gathered broadly. One person or small group decides.

Delegated

Authority given to a section lead, role, or individual.

Director-led

Director or appointed lead decides. Speed or expertise required.

← Most shared ownershipMost directed →

Together: place common ensemble decisions into these four categories.

Goal isn't to assign every decision permanently — it's to practise the distinction. Good productive tension: where does "interpretation" sit? Probably director-led in a single rehearsal but consultative across a project arc. WATCH FOR: whether the group can separate process from content.

Examples to sort

Repertoire selectionWhat pieces to perform, and why
Programming orderSequence of works in a concert or set
InterpretationTempo, phrasing, dynamics, diction, style
Ensemble rolesWho leads sections or cues entries
Rehearsal prioritiesWhat to focus on and what can wait
SchedulingRehearsal times, call times, deadlines
Performance formatFormation, staging, movement, use of space
Communication normsHow feedback is given and disagreement handled
MembershipWho joins, covers absences, substitutes
DocumentationScores, notes, shared rehearsal records
Don't rush to correct placements. Disagreement about where something sits is productive — it surfaces assumptions about who holds authority. Print this as a reference card for participants if helpful.

Activity 3  ·  20 min

Scenario testing

Choose 2–3 realistic scenarios

Repertoire choice  ·  Programme order  ·  Stage layout  ·  Contested artistic interpretation

Work through each one

Who decides?  ·  How is feedback gathered?  ·  How is disagreement handled?

Ask
  • If this happened tomorrow — who speaks, who decides, how is dissent handled?
  • What would make the process feel fair?
This is the richest phase. Let it run over if needed — shorten the consensus check instead. WATCH FOR: confusion about who holds the final call, and whether power imbalances surface. Push concretely: "This rehearsal. Tomorrow. Who picks up the baton?"
Activity 4  ·  15 min

Consensus check

✊FistCannot support
☝️1–2Major concerns
🤟3Can live with it
🖖4Cautious support
🖐️5Full agreement
Ask
  • Can you live with this process for a trial period?
  • What would you need to feel safe using it?
Capture
  • Full agreements
  • Cautious support with conditions
  • Unresolved concerns
A fist is not a failure — it's information. Surface it: "Can you say more about what's behind the fist?" Don't push for 5s. A room of 3s that feels heard is healthier than a room of 5s that felt pressured.
Closing  ·  10 min

Working agreement + next steps

Before people leave

Summarize agreed principles  ·  Name open questions  ·  Assign someone to draft the working agreement  ·  Set a specific review date

Ask
  • What have we agreed today?
  • What still needs refinement after we try this in practice?
Assign the working agreement drafter BEFORE people leave — vague shared responsibility means no document. The review date must be specific: a rehearsal date, not "in a few weeks."
Closing

From this session, we now have a draft way of working: what we decide together, what we delegate, and how we handle disagreement. We will test it, revise it, and return to it with evidence from practice.

Deliver slowly. Don't rush to pack up. After you say this, stay quiet for five seconds. Let it land.
